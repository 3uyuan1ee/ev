EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
This study explores the total cost of ownership (TCO) of electric, petrol, diesel, and hybrid
passenger cars, building on previous ERM analysis performed in 2021.1 This study quantifies the
cost to the consumer of electric and petrol cars, for first- to third-hand buyers. This study
considers the average cost of ownership of a vehicle in the EU, accounting for vehicle leasing
costs and fuel/electricity prices, whilst excluding maintenance, insurance, and country-specific
taxes and subsidies. The results of this study show that battery electric vehicles (BEVs) are
already the lowest cost option for used vehicle buyers and will be the most economical
for new buyers of medium cars from 2026 onwards.

Seven key themes have emerged from this study:

    1. Electric cars will reach the same total cost of ownership as petrol cars in 2026
         for a new medium car

    2. Electric cars are already cheaper than petrol cars for used car owners across all
         segments (small, medium, large)

    3. Access to low-cost charging is imperative for the affordability of electric cars

    4. Lower residual value of electric cars removes cost savings for their first owners

    5. Electric-only car models are cheaper than electric variants of petrol/diesel cars

    6. E-fuels will be too expensive and inconvenient for most consumers

    7. Vehicle-to-grid (V2G) technology will benefit some electric vehicle drivers but
         will not significantly improve their affordability for most users

These key findings were used to highlight areas of policy crucial for ensuring all consumers can
benefit from the lower costs of electric vehicles.

1. ELECTRIC CARS WILL REACH TOTAL COST OF OWNERSHIP PARITY WITH PETROL
    CARS IN 2026 FOR A NEW MEDIUM CAR
Total cost of ownership parity between new electric and petrol cars will be achieved
in the next two years for medium-sized cars. This is due to lower cost electric cars entering
the market, the expected increase in their residual value, and the fuel cost savings that electric
cars provide compared to petrol vehicles. Medium electric cars reach price parity first, in
2026, with small and large cars following suit in 2029 and 2032, respectively.

ELECTRIC CAR COMPARED TO A NEW PETROL CAR FOR SMALL, MEDIUM, AND LARGE CARS
BASED ON 15,000 KM ANNUAL MILEAGE. TAXES, MAINTENANCE, INSURANCE AND
SUBSISIDIES NOT INCLUDED.

As shown in Figure 0-1, the total cost of ownership advantage of electric cars is most pronounced
for small and medium cars. Encouraging the sales of new electric cars in these segments would
achieve cost savings for consumers quickest.

First owners play an important role in deciding the composition of the used car market, and the
shift to larger company cars risks reducing the choice for used car buyers looking for a smaller
car. To move towards electric cars which are affordable and desirable to all users, new smaller
electric cars must be purchased so that used car owners have sufficient choice and can benefit
from the associated cost savings. Policy support may be used to incentivise the purchase
of small and medium-sized electric cars that are desired on the second-hand market
so used car owners can also capitalise on the cost advantages of electric cars.

2. ELECTRIC CARS ARE ALREADY CHEAPER THAN PETROL CARS FOR USED CAR
    OWNERS ACROSS ALL SEGMENTS
Total cost of ownership parity is already met for second- and third- owners, given the low price
of used electric cars (due to their lower current residual value) and significant running costs
savings compared to petrol cars. Used electric cars are significantly less expensive than all other
powertrains from 2024 onwards. New electric cars registered now will deliver between
€262 and €849/year savings for their future second and third owners compared to an
equivalent petrol car.

3. ACCESS TO LOW-COST CHARGING IS IMPERATIVE FOR THE AFFORDABILITY OF
    ELECTRIC CARS
Total cost of ownership parity for first owners of electric cars is delayed by at least 6
years for all owners if they cannot access low-cost electricity by charging at home or
work, as shown in Figure 0-3 for medium-sized vehicles. Charging only at public charge points
increases the overall total cost of ownership of electric cars by 5-8% for first owners, 12-16%
for second owners, and 11-16% for third owners, depending on vehicle size. For first owners of
small and large cars, total cost of ownership parity is not reached by 2040 in this
scenario. This demonstrates the important role that providers of charging infrastructure can
have in dictating the affordability of electric cars. It also highlights that running costs affect used
car owners by a greater factor than new car owners.




FIGURE 0-3 – ILLUSTRATION OF THE DELAY IN TOTAL COST OF OWNERSHIP PARITY DUE TO
CONSUMERS ONLY HAVING ACCESS TO PUBLIC CHARGING FOR MEDIUM-SIZED CARS

Public institutions should ensure maximum deployment of home and workplace charging, paired
with encouraging competition and monitoring of price structures at public charging stations, so
those reliant on the public network do not miss out on the cost savings of electric cars.

4. LOWER RESIDUAL VALUE OF ELECTRIC CARS REMOVES COST SAVINGS FOR THEIR
    FIRST OWNERS
Today, residual value of electric cars is much lower than petrol vehicles. This study performed a
sensitivity analysis scenario in which the residual value of electric cars does not improve from
today until 2030 (the Alternative Residual Value scenario). If the residual value of electric
cars does not improve, electric cars remain more expensive than petrol cars for first
owners up to at least 2040, as demonstrated for medium cars in Figure 0-4.




FIGURE 0-4 – ANNUAL TOTAL COST OF OWNERSHIP (OVER 4 YEARS) FOR THE FIRST OWNER
OF A MEDIUM CAR IN THE ALTERNATIVE RESIDUAL VALUE SCENARIO, COMPARED TO THE
BASELINE SCENARIO

Total cost of ownership parity for first owners of electric cars is heavily reliant on the second-
hand electric car market normalising (i.e., aligning with petrol and diesel cars’ second-hand
market). If this does not occur, in a worst-case situation, total cost of ownership parity could be
pushed beyond 2040 for all vehicle segments. Policy and public information can play a crucial
role to give confidence to the market to improve second-hand residual values for BEVs.

5. ELECTRIC-ONLY CAR MODELS ARE CHEAPER THAN ELECTRIC VARIANTS OF
    PETROL/DIESEL CARS
For three of the four specific car pairs investigated, electric cars are more expensive than their
comparable petrol version for first owners (16-26%) due to the significantly higher purchase
price and lower residual value for these electric models compared to a petrol car. The exception
is the Tesla Model Y electric vehicle, which is less expensive than a Skoda Kodiaq petrol car for
first owners in 2024 due to having a favourable leasing deal compared to the Skoda despite its
similar retail price. Cheaper electric-only models coming to the market in the next year may
reduce purchase price and financing costs of electric vehicles from 2025, improving the outlook
for first buyers of vehicles outside the large segments.

Electric cars are either cheaper than their petrol counterpart (1-24%) or only marginally more
expensive for used car buyers (<6%) (see Figure 0-5 which shows total cost of ownership for
second buyers). This underscores the opportunity for cost savings for second and third buyers
who are generally more price-sensitive than new car buyers. As the price of new electric cars
decreases and OEMs incentivise their sale, the supply of electric cars into the used market will
increase, thereby increasing the available cost savings to these consumers.

6. E-FUELS WILL BE TOO EXPENSIVE AND INCONVENIENT FOR MOST CONSUMERS
E-fuels are renewable fuels of non-biological origin that have the same properties as conventional
liquid petrol or diesel. They are expensive to use and have many practical issues for deployment,
increasing the cost of running a petrol car by 9% and representing a 20% greater total cost of
ownership than a battery electric car purchased in the same year for first owners (demonstrated
in Figure 0-6). The relative cost increase of e-fuels is even greater for second and third owners,
for whom the cost increase is 36% and 40%, respectively.




FIGURE 0-6 - ADDITIONAL COST OF RUNNING A MEDIUM CAR E-FUELS COMPARED
AGAINST PETROL AND AN ELECTRIC CAR, FOR A CAR FIRST REGISTERED IN 2035

On top of the higher cost of using e-fuels, there are also other considerations which will make
e-fuels an impractical solution for both consumers, manufacturers and policy makers. For the
consumer, in addition to the higher prices, the low predicted volume of supply of e-fuels for road
transport means the number of fuelling stations will be small, limiting consumers’ autonomy and
range. For the manufacturers, these challenges include the likely requirement for OEMs to
develop and commercialise technology to chemically distinguish between fossil fuels and e-fuels
(which is currently very challenging to do given their similarity), which will increase the cost of
the car. For policymakers, the 6- to 9-fold increase in renewable energy demand to produce e-
fuels to power a car compared to directly powering an electric car will divert this renewable
energy away from reducing emissions in other sectors, such as power generation, industry or
housing. This creates a challenge for government to reach national emission reduction targets.

Due to the costs and the practical considerations, e-fuels will not be a cost-effective
way to achieve low emissions car transport from 2035 onwards. Policy to incentivise the
uptake of electric vehicles and the charging network will be significantly more cost-effective to
governments than subsidising e-fuels for road transport.

7. VEHICLE-TO-GRID (V2G) TECHNOLOGY WILL BENEFIT SOME ELECTRIC VEHICLE
    DRIVERS BUT WILL NOT SIGNIFICANTLY IMPROVE THEIR AFFORDABILITY FOR
    MOST USERS
Vehicle-to-grid (V2G) services refers to the case when an electric vehicle can both charge from
and discharge back to the electricity network. V2G could provide extra revenue to electric
car owners but relying on this to improve total cost of ownership will lead to
inequalities in electric vehicle deployment as the possible revenue is variable and highly
dependent on policy and the electricity network status.

POLICY FOCUS AREAS
As a result of these outputs, the following policy areas have been identified as important for the
future affordability of electric vehicles for consumers:

•    Provide policy stability to the new and used electric car market. This can be achieved
     with clear and consistent policies encouraging the reduction of car emissions and the
     purchase of smaller cars. Maintaining the EU’s lower CO2 target in 2025 (as well as in 2030
     and 2035) is a clear demonstration of this, which incentivises OEMs to produce more cost-
     competitive BEVs to capture more of the market. In addition, policies designed to support
     the purchase of smaller cars of all powertrains (e.g. increased taxes on heavier cars) will
     reduce the cost to consumers and increase the supply of these affordable cars on the second-
     hand market, as well as reducing emissions and improving road safety.
•    Support second-hand demand for electric cars to support the new car market. The
     largest factor to influence the cost comparison of new cars was their resale value at the end
     of the ownership period. Bolstering electric vehicle demand in the used car market will
     increase the resale value of these cars, which will reduce the cost to the first owner, leading
     to higher numbers of electric vehicle sales in total. This could be achieved through
     government grants on used vehicles, increasing access to affordable public charging for
     those without private charging and improving consumers’ awareness of the existing public
     charging network and the wider health and environmental benefits of electric vehicles.
•    Further improve access to affordable public charging. Public charging will be essential
     for many consumers who do not have access to charging at home or work, yet the increased
     cost of this means the cost savings of switching to electric are minimal. Reducing the cost
     difference between public and private charging will enable more consumers to benefit from
     the switch, increasing demand for electric vehicles.
•    E-fuels are an expensive decarbonisation option which distract from more cost-
     effective emissions reduction policies. Any policies designed to incentivise the use of e-
     fuels in road transport will cost significantly more than encouraging the adoption of electric
     cars. Using e-fuels would require expensive fuel subsidies and would divert renewable energy
     away from decarbonising other sectors.
•    Vehicle-to-grid (V2G) technology will benefit some electric vehicle drivers but
     won’t significantly improve their affordability for most users. V2G technology has
     potential to assist electricity systems to balance supply and demand across their networks,
     but this is unlikely to provide a significant revenue to most electric vehicle users. Whilst
     encouraging OEMs to include and enable V2G technology in their cars will provide benefits,
     this will not have a substantial impact on the total cost of ownership for most users and
     should not be expected to significantly increase demand for electric cars amongst
     consumers.

1.        INTRODUCTION
1.1       CONTEXT
Transport emissions are estimated to comprise a quarter of total emissions in the EU and must
therefore be addressed as part of the EU’s ambition to be climate neutral by 2050.2 Of this 25%,
passenger cars contribute over 60% of emissions, forming the largest portion of transport CO2
emissions.3 The EU has a target of zero tailpipe emissions for all newly registered cars from 2035
as well as targets to reduce tailpipe emissions by 55% by 2030 from 2022 levels,4 which will
require an accelerated transition to electric vehicles (EVs). The EV transition faces barriers such
as charging access, vehicle cost, and resale value. These issues must be understood and
addressed in policy to ensure a rapid and equitable energy transition.

On average, the growth of battery electric vehicle (BEV) deliveries has slowed in Europe since
the remarkable growth in sales observed in 2020 and 2021: according to reports by EV Volumes,
Western and Central Europe saw the smallest increase in EV sales in the first half of 2024 of any
other region.5 However, the change in EV uptake from 2023 to 2024 varies by country, dependent
on fuel/electricity prices, taxation and subsidy levels. While countries such as the Netherlands
and Norway have accelerated their EV uptake and are mandated to have 100% zero-emissions
vehicles (ZEV) sales by 2030 and 2025 respectively 6,7 (the vast majority of these being BEVs),
EV sales in Germany have declined from 2023 to 2024 after the abrupt removal of its €4,500
subsidy scheme.8 By contrast, nations with increasing policy support such as France and the UK
appear to have growing EV markets in 2024, supported by social leasing schemes and the ZEV
mandate respectively.9

1.2       PREVIOUS STUDY AND UPDATES SINCE 2021
This report was commissioned by BEUC (Bureau Européen des Unions de Consommateurs, the
European Consumer Organisation) following a previous report completed in 2021 regarding the
total cost of ownership (TCO) of vehicles for consumers over their ownership timelines.10 The
2021 analysis concluded that lifetime Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) parity between internal
combustion engine (ICE) vehicles and battery electric vehicles (BEVs) would be reached between
2022 and 2026, depending on the vehicle size, and cost savings were largest for second and
third owners of EVs. Based on an EU average across all segments, BEVs were predicted to be
cheaper for first, second, and third owners in 2025, 2023, and 2020 respectively. High mileage
drivers were determined to benefit most from a BEV by maximizing the operating cost savings.

Increasing the number of first-owner drivers purchasing BEVs also increases the affordable BEV
stock available to second and third owners.

The analysis within the report has been updated as several key parameters in the new and used
car markets have changed since 2021, as well as extending the time horizon to 2040. Key factors
in vehicle ownership costs that have varied include the cost of new and used vehicles: Autotrader
research records “exceptional” price growth in 2023 for used petrol and diesel ICE vehicles,
whilst the growth in BEV residual values has been more muted (this is discussed further in
Section 2.1).11 Additionally, the Russian invasion of Ukraine caused European energy prices to
spike in 2022-23, which have since started to fall partly due to emergency mitigation measures
across the EU.12 High electricity prices reduced the operating cost benefit of running a BEV during
this time, potentially affecting consumers’ willingness to take on the higher upfront cost of a BEV
compared to an ICE car.

1.3       OBJECTIVES
This report aims to highlight the changes in TCO of electric vehicles and other powertrains in
Europe since 2021, identifying where these differences arise and why they have occurred. This
analysis is intended for use as a resource to inform policy development in the EU regarding
emissions reduction from passenger vehicles.

1.4       STRUCTURE OF THE REPORT
This report is broken down into six sections (including this one), then followed by appendices
with supporting information for the results discussed in this report. The six sections are:

1. Introduction (this section): Introduces the aims of this report and the wider context of
     this report in relation to EU legislation and the current state of the new and used car markets.
2. Cost of ownership from 2024 to 2040 (Page 13): This section presents the cost of
     ownership for first, second, and third hand owners for a consumer in a hypothetical “EU
     average” country. This cost of ownership includes the vehicle cost (including financing costs),
     fuel or electricity costs, and the cost of installing a charger at home for those who can. This
     is based on ownership periods of 4, 5, and 7 years for first, second, and third owners
     respectively. First owners are assumed to cover the greatest annual mileage at
     15,000km/year, with second and third owners covering 12,000km/year and 10,000km/year,
     respectively. Further details about the inputs used can be found in Appendix A.
3. Sensitivity of the TCO results (Page 21): This section explores the impact residual values

new car. Importantly, the gap between petrol ICE and BEV has increased slightly over this time,

3.2.5 CONCLUSIONS OF SCENARIO ANALYSIS
The key conclusions of this sensitivity are as follows:

    •    Lack of home and workplace charging severely reduce the value proposition of a BEV
         from a TCO perspective and means price parity of BEVs and ICE vehicles is not reached
         as quickly or is not reached at all. Used car buyers are more likely to be affected by
         higher public charging costs because they rely more heavily on public charging. These
         consumers are more likely to live in apartments, and therefore are less likely to have
         access to a private garage where they can charge at home.

4.1.3 THIRD BUYERS

charge more (or exclusively) at home or work or the price of public charging decreases, their

models from 2025 onwards (such as the Renault R5, Citroën E-C3, and Volkswagen ID2), these

TCO of a BEV being lower than a petrol ICE for all vehicle sizes, not just the larger segments.

owners, with most of this difference being attributable to financing costs. Charging costs are
approximately equal across all electric vehicle models studied, given the relatively low mileage

5.        OTHER FACTORS THAT MAY INFLUENCE VEHICLE TCO
Whilst this TCO study covers the main costs that will be incurred by current and future car users,
there are additional considerations which could influence the TCO of using e-fuels or BEVs, which
are likely to be the only two options for new cars in the EU beyond 2035.

5.1       ADDITIONAL PRACTICAL CONSIDERATIONS OF E-FUEL USE
In addition to the increased cost of e-fuels compared to petrol or diesel, there are other practical
factors that could either impact the TCO or the attractiveness of using e-fuels to consumers.
There are three broad areas to consider: the requirement for additional technology in cars to
ensure only e-fuels are used, the ease of sourcing e-fuels, and the wider impact on electricity
supply within the EU.

From 2035, OEMs must have achieved a 100% reduction in carbon intensity of running new cars
sold (i.e. 0 gCO2/km),35 which currently does not include low carbon fuels such as e-fuels.
However, the EU commission may decide to allow e-fuels to be used in new cars from 2035
onwards. This would likely require OEMs to install technology into these new cars to detect
whether the vehicle has been filled up with e-fuel or fossil fuel and reject the use of fossil fuels.36
The technology would need to distinguish between low carbon e-fuels and other disallowed fuels
produced by very similar processes, such as biofuels and fuels produced through the Fischer-
Tropsch process from fossil fuel feedstocks,37 which could be extremely difficult. Any technology
developed will likely increase the cost of these vehicles. The magnitude of this impact on the
TCO is highly uncertain, as the requirements under EU legislation and the technology that would
be required are currently uncertain.

Importantly, no onboard technology will be able to verify the sustainability of the fuel used,
which needs to be done through separate analysis and verified by a third party. This is currently
done for renewable fuels supplied to fulfil Member State targets to meet the renewable energy
in transport targets of the Renewable Energy Directive. However, the EU’s policy here is to verify
the sustainability of fuels that are used to comply with policy support mechanisms, not to ensure
that non-compliant fuels are prevented from being sold and used. Today the risk of this is low:
biofuels and e-fuels are more expensive than fossil fuels, so there is no incentive for
unsustainable ones to be supplied without policy support. However, if a fleet of e-fuel only cars
were on the road, there would be a strong incentive for sale of fuels that would be accepted by
the onboard technology but could be supplied at lower cost by avoiding sustainability
requirements.

finding locations to refuel. ReFuelEU aviation legislation mandates the use of sustainable fuels
for aviation to 2050,38 including sub targets for e-fuel use. This mandate will require about 16
million tonnes of sustainable aviation fuels in the EU, 4.8 million tonnes of which would be e-

5.2       POTENTIAL IMPACT OF V2G ON TCO

5.2.1 OVERVIEW OF V2G
Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G), also known as bidirectional charging, allows EVs to both charge from the
grid and discharge to the grid. V2X is the overarching term for services provided using
bidirectional charging, where the X stands for any application, for instance grid, home, building
(V2G, V2H, V2B).
Participation in V2G services can provide benefits to EV owners by enabling an EV battery to act
as a short duration energy store. The first benefit is increased revenue from energy arbitrage:
V2G can charge when energy is cheap and discharge onto the grid when energy is at its most
expensive (see Figure 5-3). In addition, EVs can charge during times of abundant (i.e.
renewable) supply and deliver this back into the grid when demand is high in the place of a
carbon-intensive gas power plant, reducing the CO2 emissions of a household and the grid. EVs
may also be able to participate in local flexibility services in the future. This is discussed further
in section 5.2.2.

5.2.2 REVENUE POTENTIAL FROM V2G
V2G can offer electricity system and financial benefits by allowing vehicles to participate in
electricity markets. Not all services are suitable, however, for V2G. For example, frequency
response45 is often assessed as not suitable for V2G, due to the metering and latency
requirements.44 For the typical usage patterns and plug-in times of passenger cars, they can
offer a surplus of energy when plugged in overnight, which can be used for engagement in
wholesale energy arbitrage (discharging electricity to the grid at times of high electricity prices),
the Balancing Mechanism and local flexibility markets (helping to prevent congestion in the grid
by discharging electricity). These V2G activities can generate value for car owners, though the
size of the benefit will vary. Notably, the value of local flexibility markets can be highly variable
as they are very locationally dependent, and not everyone may be able to access the full value.

5.2.3 BARRIERS TO V2G ADOPTION
Despite these advantages, there are several barriers to the implementation of V2G, though many
of these are likely to reduce over the next few years.

•    Charger cost: The bi-directional chargers required for V2G can be three times as expensive
     than an average smart charger (premium of €1,000-€3,300),48 though this price difference
     is likely to reduce over time if the technology becomes more widespread (€350-1,000
     premium by 2030). In addition, trials of V2G through lower-cost AC chargers are underway
     in the EU, which can use a cheaper bidirectional AC charger.49 In October 2024, Nissan
     announced that their AC V2G chargers will be available in the UK in 2026.50 Although
     maintenance costs are typically low for all chargers, it may rise to approximately €120 per
     year per charger (2023€) for general repairs for more complex V2G hardware.
•    Vehicle availability: There are currently very few V2G-enabled vehicles on the market (e.g.
     Nissan Leaf, Nissan eNV200, and Mitsubishi Outlander). However, more V2G-enabled
     vehicles are expected to come to market in the next 3 years with the adoption of the ISO
     15118-20 communication standard, enabling CCS (Combined Charging System) V2G, which
     is the more popular DC charging system in Europe. This includes VW ID models (already
     capable, not enabled pending ISO 15118-20 adoption), Renault R5, and Mercedes Benz EQS.
     Currently only CHAdeMO compatible charge points and cars are V2G capable.
•    Battery Degradation: Further, there are concerns around battery degradation from more
     frequent battery cycling, which could reduce the range of an EV, though the evidence for this
     and the subsequent impact on BEV range or value is currently inconclusive. If V2G is carried
     out without regard for battery degradation, it can accelerate the rate of range loss in BEVs.
     This could cost BEV owners between €100 and €1,000 per year in additional battery
     degradation, depending on how V2G is managed, the number of cycles completed, the depth
     of discharge of each cycle, and the charging speed used (see Appendix C for further
     discussion). However, it may be able to slow down this process if carried out to deliberately
     minimise, or even slow, degradation.51,52
•    Uncertain revenue: Finally, future revenues from V2G are uncertain due to the potential
     saturation of the flexibility services market with utility-scale batteries or large-scale V2G
     participation. This would reduce the monetary benefit for customers as the value of flexibility
     services would decrease.

5.2.4 IMPACT OF V2G ON TCO
V2G may improve the TCO of a small number of BEV owners (as shown in Figure 5-4), but this
is highly dependent on location, evolution of commercial offerings, and technology costs.

The demand for local flexibility services is highly contingent on the status of the local grid
(whether it regularly reaches capacity or if there is plenty of headroom). As this can vary at a
very local level, this can create an unequal distribution of V2G revenue according to postcode,
with some owners able to generate significant revenue whilst others generating next-to-none.
In addition, this is likely to only be available to owners who have access to private overnight
charging, so could exacerbate equity differences between people living in houses with off-street
parking and those in terraced housing or flats without garages.

Figure 5-4 shows a low cost, high revenue scenario (Scenario A) and a high cost, low revenue
scenario (Scenario B) for a V2G participant in Europe. This illustrates the potential benefits and
pitfalls of engaging with V2G for consumers. V2G could cost the customer an additional €1,430
per year, or alternatively may reduce the TCO of a BEV by over €600 per year. As previously
stated, this is dependent on location, technology improvement rate, and real-world battery
degradation rates.

6.      CONCLUSIONS AND POLICY IMPLICATIONS
The analysis of vehicle TCO from 2024 to 2040 shows that BEVs are cheaper across all vehicle
segments by 2032 for new car buyers and are already and always cheaper for used car buyers.
The savings available to new BEV buyers from 2030-2040 are between €240/year and €645/year
(2-7% saving), depending on segment, compared to an equivalent petrol ICE car. Used car
buyers can achieve a cost reduction of between 6% (€230/year) and 20% (€560/year) compared
to petrol ICE, depending on segment. For all consumers, running an ICE vehicle on e-fuels
bought in 2035 is more expensive than both petrol ICE and BEV, ranging from 32% more
expensive than BEV for the first owner to 36% more expensive for the second owner. Over the
lifetime of the vehicle, BEVs are €6,000 less expensive for consumers than petrol ICE cars
purchased in 2030. This clearly demonstrates the value proposition of BEVs from as early as
2024 under current market conditions.

The residual value of cars is an important factor in the TCO, and how this changes over time is
uncertain for all powertrains. Under a scenario in which the residual value of BEVs does not
improve from today’s relatively low values, BEVs are always more expensive for new buyers than
petrol ICE cars. This is in stark contrast to the baseline scenario where TCO parity is reached
between 2026 and 2032 depending on segment, in this scenario the residual value of BEVs
improves over time to match that of a diesel ICE car by 2030. In both the baseline and residual
value sensitivity scenarios, BEVs are significantly cheaper on a TCO basis than petrol ICE cars
for second and third owners, who in general are more price sensitive than first owners. In order
to ensure supply of BEVs for the second-hand market, new car buyers must also choose to buy
BEVs. Whilst the residual value is expected to improve as the market for new and used BEVs
becomes more established (i.e., the baseline scenario of this report), trends in residual value
should be closely monitored and assisted by policy if required to ensure the long-term desirability
of BEVs. Policies which improve the residual value through greater convenience and cost of using
a BEV for all (e.g. more affordable public charging, preferential tax rates) are likely to provide a
more lasting improvements to the TCO than a one-off subsidy for new car buyers.

Access to low-cost charging is an equally significant aspect of BEV affordability. When consumers
do not have access to home and workplace charging – instead relying on the public network –
only new medium BEVs reach price parity in 2032, later than in the baseline scenario, with small
and large BEVs remaining more expensive than petrol ICE in 2040. For used cars, the results
are similar, with little or no savings available to BEV owners compared to petrol ICE car owners.
The high cost of public charging means that the charging costs of BEVs in this scenario are
similar to the refuelling costs of petrol ICE cars per kilometre driven. Lack of low-cost charging
access considerably reduces the value proposition of BEVs, meaning policy intervention may be
required to address this gap.

Policymakers should endeavour to take advantage of the TCO reduction that BEVs can offer,
especially for used car buyers. A combination of measures to aid the residual value market for
BEVs and ensure access to low-cost charging may be required to maximise the TCO advantage
of BEVs over petrol ICE cars. This would in turn help to increase EV uptake and reduce road
transport emissions, which is a key aspect of reaching Net Zero by 2050 in the EU. Policy
interventions could include incentives for the installation of private chargers, measures to
accelerate the deployment of EVs in large private and public fleets, residual value guarantees or
targeted subsidies for new or used BEV, as well as increased competition and better monitoring
of price structures at public charging stations. Similarly, concerns around BEV range and safety


                CLIENT: Bureau Européen des Unions de Consommateurs (BEUC)
                PROJECT NO: 0742844        DATE: 25th Oct 2024    VERSION: 3                  Page 41
could be mitigated by governments through positive messaging of the financial, health, and
environmental benefits of BEVs to counter anti-BEV stories currently in some sections of the
media.

The current costs of four pairs of BEV and petrol ICE equivalent cars were identified for analysis
(Fiat 500 BEV and petrol ICE, Peugeot 208 BEV and petrol ICE, VW ID3 and Golf, Tesla Model Y,
and Skoda Kodiaq). For three of the four pairs (Fiat 500, Peugeot 208, VW ID.3/Golf), the TCO
for the first buyer was cheaper for petrol ICE, despite the lower cost of electricity for the BEV
compared to petrol for the ICE. This is due to the higher cost of leasing a BEV, driven by higher
purchase prices and lower residual values assumed by leasing companies, meaning a larger
proportion of car value is paid off by the consumer. In contrast, the Tesla Model Y is cheaper
than the Skoda Kodiaq, as Tesla offers a favourable deal on leasing this car. Notably, BEVs are
cheaper for second owners for all model pairs, and the BEV VW ID3 and Tesla Model Y are
cheaper than their equivalents for third owners; however, the BEV Fiat 500 and Peugeot 208 are
marginally (6%) more expensive than their petrol ICE counterparts. Costs vary significantly
between European countries and manufacturers; therefore, less expensive leases may be
possible in countries other European countries or from different manufacturers. Further, cheaper
BEV-only models are coming to the market in 2025, which will likely bring down purchase and
financing costs in the near future and will mean consumers are less reliant on more expensive
BEV versions of ICE-dominant models such as the BEV Fiat 500 and Peugeot 208.
The potential impact of V2G on TCO is difficult to predict, with estimates ranging between an
additional cost of €1,430/year and a TCO saving of €620/year based on regulations and revenue
streams, using the UK as a case study. Maximum revenue is difficult to achieve due to its
sensitivity to location: the distribution of revenue opportunity varies greatly by area and even
postcode. However, the additional costs of V2G equipment can be significant, and may outweigh
the benefits for some consumers. As such the impact of V2G on an average driver is likely to be
minimal.
Overall, whilst the TCO of BEVs has been improving compared to ICE vehicles, the current lower
residual values of BEVs and the higher cost of public charging compared to home charging could
undermine the uptake of BEVs and the cost savings seen for first, second and third owners.
Monitoring of these aspects and action where required by policy makers will be essential to
further improve BEV uptake, unlocking cost savings for car owners, reducing urban air pollution
and enabling the EU to meet their emissions reduction targets

7.4      RESIDUAL VALUE
In this report, depreciation curves were created based on ERM’s trend analysis of real-world
market data that Transport and Environment (T&E) procured as an update for “An analysis of
used car prices in Europe” (2023).59 Data for 2-, 3-, and 4-year-old vehicles driving 10, 15, and
20 thousand km per year was analysed. This was extrapolated to cover the full vehicle lifetime
(approximately 16 years). For petrol and diesel vehicles, depreciation rates are extrapolated
linearly until the residual value reaches 10% of the original purchase cost, at which point each
subsequent year’s RV is set to 95% of the previous year. BEV and PHEV depreciation rates are
significantly steeper, which if extrapolated linearly would reach 10% residual value (RV) in 8
years (BEVs) or 11 years (PHEVs) (based on 15,000 km/year), contrary to what is seen currently
in the used car market. In order to develop a more reasonable residual value curve for these
powertrains, the residual value of BEVs and PHEVs follows an exponential curve once older than
4 years, so the residual value equals 10% at the same point that Diesel ICE vehicles reach 10%
(approximately 16 years-old).

9.1      V2G OVERVIEW
There are several ways for EVs to be charged or discharged to support different functions, such
as the grid or buildings. Vehicle-Grid Integration (VGI) is the general term for whenever EV
charging is turned into a flexible load, using the battery storage to provide services to the grid
or end user. This includes V1G and Vehicle-to-Everything (V2X) applications. V2X is the
overarching term for services provided using bidirectional charging, where the X stands for any
application. A number of these applications are illustrated below in Figure 9-1:

9.2      BATTERY DEGRADATION FROM V2G
The battery degradation cost analysis seen in section 5.2.4 is based on previous work carried
out by ERM for ClimateXChange61 to understand the opportunities of V2G to accelerate transport
decarbonisation in Scotland, published in 2024. This includes the modelling of the cost of battery
degradation due to V2G participation, which can be modelled as an annual cost.

There are two forms of battery degradation:

     1. Cycling degradation: battery capacity gradually decreases with each charge/discharge
        cycle, signalling that the greater number of cycles undergone by a battery, the greater
        its degradation.

       battery is left at the limits of its state of charge (SOC) (i.e., 0% or 100% SOC).61

a loss of 0.04% of a battery’s state of health per discharge cycle, whereas the lower bound

of health per cycle. Assuming two daily cycles of a 50kWh battery (as seen in Peugeot e-208)
leads to approximately €1,200 of annual degradation costs in 2025 at the upper limit, and €175
